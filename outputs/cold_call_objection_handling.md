# PI Law Firm Cold Call Playbook — Objection Handling & Hidden Costs

*Hormozi-framework breakdown for selling the AI intake funnel to personal injury firms.*

---

## The core diagnosis

You've got a real product, real proof, and you're giving it away free. The friction you're feeling is pure **perceived risk and effort**. You already killed the **price** objection by making it free — so every "no" you're hearing is a **hidden cost** in disguise (the perceived time, risk, hassle, and ego-cost of saying *yes*). Your whole job on each call is to make "yes" feel effortless and "no" feel like a loss.

---

## OBJECTION 1: "We already respond within 3-4 minutes."

**The real fear underneath it:**
They're protecting their ego. They've built a process they're proud of. Admitting they have a gap feels like admitting failure. The deeper fear is: *"If I say yes, I'm implicitly saying my current setup is broken."*

**The reframe:**
Don't attack their 3-4 minutes. Agree with it. Then expand the frame. Their 3-4 minutes is a best-case daytime number. You're solving for the 70%+ of the week that isn't 9-to-5.

**The math that ends the conversation:**
There are 168 hours in a week. Standard business hours are roughly 45. That means **123 hours per week — 73% of all waking hours — your system is asleep and mine is alive.** That's not a marketing claim. That's arithmetic.

**Talk Track A (soft open):**
> "That's actually really impressive — 3-4 minutes is better than most firms I talk to. Quick question though: does that include Saturday at 11pm? Or Monday morning when your intake coordinator is already on two calls? Because in my experience, that's exactly when the lead is most likely to call the next firm. I'm not trying to replace what you're doing during business hours — I'm covering the hours when nobody's there to answer."

**Talk Track B (harder, if they push back):**
> "Walk me through what happens when a lead calls Friday at 7pm. Like specifically — what's the process? ... Right. So for however many hours that gap is, your competitor's system is live and yours isn't. What I built doesn't compete with your intake person. It's what happens when your intake person isn't there."

**Talk Track C (close the loop):**
> "If I could show you that you're losing leads specifically in those off-hours windows — and that my system catches them automatically, qualifies them, and books the consult before they call anyone else — would you want to see the data? Because we can actually track that."

---

## OBJECTION 2: "We're meticulous about who we take on — an automated text isn't good enough."

**The real fear underneath it:**
Two fears stacked on top of each other.
- **Fear one:** "A robot will embarrass me in front of an injured person who deserves compassion." They picture a cold, robotic message going to someone who just got hit by a truck and is scared.
- **Fear two:** "If the AI screws up the intake, I could take a bad case, miss a good case, or worse — violate a bar rule." They conflate intake automation with case evaluation.

**The reframe:**
They're describing a manual process that is *less* consistent than what you're offering. Their meticulous process depends on whoever answers the phone that day. Your system applies their exact criteria every single time, without variance. And critically — the AI does NOT evaluate cases. It's a routing system. **It's a triage nurse, not the doctor.**

**Talk Track A (empathy first, then flip):**
> "I completely agree with you — the intake call for an injured person should be handled carefully. And I want to be clear about what this system actually does, because I think there might be a misunderstanding. The AI doesn't evaluate cases. It doesn't give opinions. It doesn't tell anyone whether they have a good case. All it does is collect the basic intake information, check if the minimum criteria are met — like is the incident recent, was there a doctor visit, is there an at-fault party — and if yes, it books a consult slot on your calendar and sends a confirmation. Your attorney still makes every decision about whether to take the case. I'm not touching that part at all."

**Talk Track B (the consistency angle):**
> "Can I ask — when your intake coordinator does the first call, do they apply the same criteria every single time? Because what I've found is that the variance in human intake is actually where good cases get lost. Someone calls at the end of the day when the coordinator is tired, or a lead comes in on a Sunday and nobody sees it until Tuesday. This system doesn't get tired. It doesn't have a bad day. It applies your exact criteria every single time. If anything, it makes your meticulous process more consistent."

**Talk Track C (the compliance kill shot):**
> "The system is specifically designed to NOT give legal advice. It's a guardrail I built in from the beginning because I knew this concern would come up. It qualifies and schedules. That's it. Think of it like a very smart intake form that also books the appointment. Your attorney does all the actual evaluation on the consult. I can show you the exact message templates if you want to review them before we go live."

## HIDDEN COSTS — AND HOW TO ABSORB EVERY ONE

Here's what's running through their head even if they don't say it out loud. The move is the same every time: **you eat the cost so their effort/risk drops to ~zero.**

**1. Setup time / IT hassle** — *"I'll have to figure out how to connect this to my calendar, my CRM, my website..."*
> "I set everything up for you. You give me access to your Google Calendar and tell me your intake criteria. I handle the rest. You don't touch any tech."

**2. Learning curve** — *"My staff will have to learn a new system."*
> "There's nothing for your staff to learn. Leads come in, they get qualified, they show up on your calendar. That's what your team sees. Nothing changes on their end."

**3. Embarrassment risk** — *"What if it sends a weird message to a client and they screenshot it on Google Reviews?"*
> "Before we go live, you review every single message template. You approve the exact wording. Nothing goes out that you haven't signed off on. If you don't like a single word, we change it."

**4. Bar / ethics / compliance risk** — *"What if this violates attorney advertising or unauthorized-practice rules?"*
> "The system never gives legal advice, never assesses case merit, and never makes representations on behalf of the firm. It collects information and schedules appointments — the same function a receptionist performs. I can walk you through the compliance design with your bar's rules in mind."

**5. Data / privacy concerns** — *"Client intake data is sensitive. Where does it go? Who sees it?"*
> "All data stays in YOUR systems — your Google Calendar, your email, your spreadsheet or CRM. I don't store client data on my end. I can show you the data path before we turn anything on."

**6. "One more thing to manage"** — *"I already have too many tools."*
> "Once it's live, you don't manage it. It runs. If something breaks, I fix it. I'm your tech support. This is my job, not yours."

**7. Fear of looking replaceable** — *"My staff will think their jobs are threatened."*
> "This takes the grind off your intake coordinator — the after-hours calls, the weekend voicemails, the 'I forgot to log that lead.' They get to focus on the consults that actually matter. It's a tool for them, not a replacement."

**8. Switching cost from current answering service** — *"We pay for an answering service for after-hours."*
> "What does that service cost per month? Because this is free, it's faster, it qualifies automatically, and it books directly to the calendar. You might actually be able to cut that cost."

## VALUE PROP REWRITE (Value Equation)

**(Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)** — max the top, drive the bottom to zero.

- **Dream Outcome:** Never lose a PI case because you were the second firm to call back.
- **Perceived Likelihood:** System is live and proven. I'll show you a demo before you commit to anything.
- **Time Delay:** Live in days, not weeks.
- **Effort & Sacrifice:** Zero. I build it. I set it up. You approve the messages. Done.

**One-liner:**
> "I built a system that qualifies your PI leads and books consults in under 60 seconds — 24/7 — so you never lose a case to the firm that called back first."

**Cold call opener (~30 seconds):**
> "Hey [name], quick question — when a lead submits a contact form at 9pm on a Sunday, what happens to it at your firm? ... Yeah, that's what I figured. I built a system that qualifies that lead and books them directly on the attorney's calendar in under 60 seconds, no matter what time it comes in. It's AI-powered, it never gives legal advice, and I'm setting it up for free right now for a handful of firms to get case studies. Is that worth 10 minutes to see how it works?"

## The meta-point

Their "no" isn't about the product. It's about the **effort of saying yes.** Your job on every call is to reduce the cost of agreeing. Every objection is a hidden cost in disguise. You've already solved the price problem by making it free. Now you're in the business of making "yes" feel effortless and "no" feel like a loss.

**Volume negates luck.** Get on 50 calls. The talk tracks sharpen themselves.
